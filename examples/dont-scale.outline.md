# Startups Don't Start Themselves

Here's the mental model that changes everything: a startup is not a seed you plant and water. At the very beginning it has no momentum of its own. The founders are the engine that gets it moving — by sheer manual force — until it can finally run on its own.

Nothing about the early days is automatic. The growth you eventually want to be self-sustaining has to first be created by hand.

> Like **hand-cranking an old car engine** — you spin it by force until the motor catches and runs on its own. No crank, no ride.

---

<!-- slide id=s_fa0nqri8 layout=plain -->
# The First Unscalable Move: Recruit Users by Hand

If founders must manually push, what does that actually look like? Most often, it's getting users **one at a time, in person**.

- You don't wait for users to discover you — you go to them.
- Stripe's founders would walk people through setup on the spot ('the Collison installation').
- Airbnb's founders went door-to-door in New York to sign up hosts.

This feels backwards. It looks like the opposite of building a scalable business. That's exactly why it works — and why almost no one does it.

---

<!-- slide id=s_nvgjvi7g layout=plain -->
# Why Founders Resist

If recruiting users by hand works so well, why don't founders do it? The resistance is the heart of the story.

- **Temperament:** many founders are shy hackers who'd rather build than talk to strangers. Cranking the engine feels uncomfortable and exhausting.
- **The numbers look pathetic:** signing up users one by one yields tiny absolute totals. It feels like it can't possibly matter.

Both instincts are traps. The discomfort and the small numbers are not signs you're doing it wrong — they're the normal texture of the earliest days.

<!-- slide id=s_s65qftb4 layout=analogy -->
# The Math Founders Underestimate

Those tiny numbers feel like failure because founders judge them as a snapshot instead of a rate. The thing that matters isn't *how many* users today — it's *how fast the number compounds*.

At 10% growth per week, 100 users becomes millions within about two years. The early total is supposed to look small; the slope is what you're building.

> Like **a single fold of paper** — one fold seems trivial, but fold it 42 times and it would reach the moon. Compounding hides enormous outcomes inside unimpressive beginnings.

---

<!-- slide id=s_vekib1on layout=analogy -->
# Fragile Things Get Misjudged

Because early numbers are tiny, outsiders (and founders themselves) judge a startup by the standards of established companies — and conclude it's a toy. But nearly every startup is extremely fragile at the start. That fragility is normal, not fatal.

The right question isn't 'how big is this now?' It's 'how big could this get if the founders did the right things?'

> Like **judging an oak by the acorn** — measured as it is today, it looks like nothing. Measured by what it could become, it's a forest.

---

<!-- slide id=s_trkk8jdf layout=analogy -->
# Delight: Be Insanely Great, Not Just Build It

Manual effort isn't only about acquiring users — it's about overwhelming the ones you have. Take extraordinary, unscalable measures to delight them: hand-written notes, personal attention, a level of care big companies literally cannot afford to give.

Notice the reframe: aim for an insanely great *experience*, not necessarily an insanely great *product*. Attentiveness can cover for software that's still early, buggy, and incomplete — and that close contact is also your best feedback loop for making the product genuinely good.

> Like **a tiny family restaurant** where the owner remembers your name — the food may be simple, but the care makes it unforgettable in a way no chain can match.

---

<!-- slide id=s_fah1hxd3 layout=analogy -->
# Start a Fire in a Small Space

Hand-cranking only works if your effort is concentrated. Trying to delight everyone everywhere spreads you too thin to reach critical mass. So begin in a deliberately narrow market, dominate it, then expand.

Facebook launched only at Harvard — a small enough world to fully win before moving on.

> Like **building a campfire** — you get a small pile of kindling blazing hot first, *then* add the big logs. Scatter the flame across wet wood and it just dies out.

---

<!-- slide id=s_rb9s0oum layout=plain -->
# The Idea Is a Vector, Not a Point

Pulling it all together: a startup idea isn't just *what you'll build*. It's a vector with two parts — **what you'll build** plus **the unscalable things you'll do to get it started**.

- Hardware founders can assemble the first units by hand ('pull a Meraki').
- B2B founders can act as consultants, building for a single user, or do tasks manually before automating them.
- Beware the strategies that omit real effort — the Big Launch, the big-company partnership. Any plan that lets you skip the cranking is suspect.

Ideally, those early unscalable habits don't disappear once you scale — they become permanent DNA: a company that stays unusually attentive because that's how it learned to live.
