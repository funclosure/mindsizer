Resistance · The Heart Of The Story

Why Founders Resist

If recruiting users by hand works so well — why doesn't every founder do it? Two instincts say stop. Both are traps.

01 / Temperament
Shy hackers would rather build than talk to strangers.
Cranking the engine by hand feels uncomfortable and exhausting — the opposite of why you became a hacker.
The trapDiscomfort isn't a sign you're doing it wrong.
02 / The Numbers
Signing up users one by one yields tiny totals.
A handful of names in a spreadsheet. It feels like it can't possibly matter at the scale you're imagining.
The trapSmall numbers are a seed, not a verdict.
What the instincts conclude Both signals point the same way: this is beneath you, and it isn't working. Quit cranking. The discomfort and the small numbers are not signs you're doing it wrong — they're the normal texture of the earliest days.
THE MATH FOUNDERS UNDERESTIMATE  ·  COMPOUNDING

Tiny numbers feel like failure — because you read the snapshot, not the slope.

It isn't how many users today. It's how fast the number compounds.

At 10% growth per week, 100 users becomes millions in about two years. The early total is supposed to look small. The slope is the thing you're really building.

Unscalable  ·  The Reframe
Delight: be insanely great, not just build it

Manual effort isn't only about acquiring users — it's about overwhelming the ones you have. Hand-written notes, personal attention, a level of care big companies literally cannot afford.

Aim for an insanely great experience, not necessarily an insanely great product. Attentiveness covers for software that's still early and buggy — and that contact is your best feedback loop.

Like a tiny family restaurant where the owner remembers your name — the food may be simple, but the care makes it unforgettable in a way no chain can match.

The Idea Is a Vector, Not a Point
An idea isn't just what you'll build. It's a vector — what you'll build plus the unscalable things you do to get it started. Drop the second component and the idea collapses back to a point.

Ideally those unscalable habits never disappear once you scale — they become permanent DNA: a company that stays unusually attentive because that's how it learned to live.
